Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Matthew Mitchell — mam084@ucsd.edu

Proposition

The energy sector is the primary driver of national emissions growth.

FOR the Proposition

Visualization A — “Energy and Manufacturing Lead Surge in Emissions”

Design decisions & rationale

  • -1.0 Tight y-limits with minimal padding. Reducing headroom amplifies bar height differences year-over-year, making growth appear more dramatic without clipping data. I considered a full 0-baseline with generous padding, but it made changes look muted.
  • +1.0 Consistent sector ordering and grouped bars for 2010→2024. This preserves comparability and reduces scanning load, helping viewers quickly spot the Energy trend. Alternatives like stacked bars obscured year-to-year growth and were very cluttered, reducing readability.
  • -1.5 Emphasis in title and caption. The wording primes the audience to focus on Energy’s rise. A more neutral title would be less persuasive.
  • +1.0 Uniform color set by year. Keeping hues constant by year (instead of by sector) makes within-sector growth pop across time. Coloring sectors differently pulled attention sideways across categories instead of forward in time.
  • -0.5 Wide aspect ratio. Stretching horizontally increases separation between year groups and visually enhances upward changes. A squarer plot condenses the graph and reduces the visual difference.

AGAINST the Proposition

Visualization B — “Energy’s Share of Total Emissions Has Stayed Consistently Low”

Design decisions & rationale

  • -1.5 Logarithmic y-axis. Log scale compresses the space between large values and stabilizes variation, making Energy look proportionally small. A linear axis made the gap feel smaller and less supportive of our claim.
  • -0.5 Subdued color for totals; brighter accent for Energy. Paradoxically, the accent draws the eye to small bars, reinforcing that they’re consistently much shorter than totals. A vivid color for totals would dominate attention.
  • +0.5 Side-by-side bars with identical category order. This allows fair comparison across the same years. Alternatives like stacked bars hid absolute differences.
  • -0.5 Calm grid and generous margins. Extra whitespace and light gridlines reduce a sense of volatility. A cramped layout read as “busy” and emphasized fluctuations.
  • +1.0 Explicit legend (“Category”). Clear labeling prevents confusion about which bars represent totals versus the subset, reducing accidental misreadings.

Final Reflection

Designing persuasive visuals for both sides of a single proposition clarified how framing, scale, and text steer interpretation even when the underlying data and encodings remain reasonable. The “FOR” figure relied on layout and range choices that magnify differences over time within a single sector, whereas the “AGAINST” figure leaned on a logarithmic scale and calm styling to keep Energy in proportion to the national total. Neither graphic fabricates data, but each one definitely encourages a distinct conclusion.

The most straightforward steps were data transformation and consistent ordering. The difficult parts were deciding how far to push scaling and titles without crossing into obvious manipulation. I was surprised by how strongly extending and padding axes affected perceived growth. Small changes to whitespace can shift the narrative even before scaling changes or annotations.

I define ethical analysis and visualization as faithfully representing data and uncertainty while choosing designs that are relevant to the question and being candid about consequential decisions. Acceptable persuasion is a truthful reading of the data (e.g., consistent grouping, clear legends, and transparent transformations). What I consider “misleading” is when scale choices, filtering, or loaded language intentionally hide important information a reasonable viewer would need to reach an informed conclusion. The boundary isn't exactly a solid line, but intent and disclosure are key pieces.
